Western Visayas

BINAKOE/BINAKUL

Malay, Aklan

Traditional binakoe is cooked inside a fresh node of bamboo. Native chicken plus onions and lemongrass are added. For the souring agent, aeabihig leaves complete the mix. Think of this dish as similar to the sinigang and might appeal to Tagalog palates. A hot plate of rice plus steaming binakoe then paired with an ice cold soda would be the perfect lunch!

Linapay

Kalibo, Aklan

Linapay is the Aklanon ‘laing’ pounded small shrimps (not alamang) seasoned with garlic, onion, ginger, chili, and tender ‘batwan’ fruit wrapped in young ‘gabi’ leaves and cooked in coconut milk. In cooking this dish, the difference is that ‘ueang’ (pounded small shrimps), ‘gawud’ coconut meat, wrapped in fresh taro leaves then cook in coconut milk. Its ingredients are abundant anywhere in Aklan.

Linapwahan

Tibiao, Antique

it is a Filipino dish prepared with a different mix of vegetables and the most popular ones are moringa (malunggay leaves), drumsticks (malunggay fruit), green papaya, jute leaves (saluyot), okra, Malabar spinach (alugbati), squash, string beans, bitter gourd (ampalaya), eggplant and spinach. It is then flavoured with ginamus / fermented anchovy sauce plus some seafood in the form of fresh water shrimps (orang), river crabs (kalampay), dried shrimp fry (kalkag), dried anchovies (baog) and/or dried fish (pinakas). All of these wonderful ingredients are then boiled simply in water with tomatoes and onions and trust me it creates this wonderful vegetable stew that is slightly sweet, rich and tons of umami.

Pasta Porbida

Sibalom, Antique

Porbida is a special dish introduced by the Spanish colonizers to the locale people in Sibalom and San Remigio towns which they served to their guests. Pasta Porbida is cooked by sautéing onion with achuete oil, garlic, native chicken cutlets, and adding chicken broth and Alupidan leaves (Tetrastigma harmandii) that are abundant in the municipalities of Sibalom and San Remigio. Coconut milk is also being added towards the end of the cooking process to make the dish creamy and tangy for people to love eating it.

Diwal Pasta

Roxas City, Capiz

Diwal or angel wing clams is one of the prized shellfish specialties in Capiz. Locals say that the meat is sweet and succulent with a taste superior to oysters and mussels and a more delicate texture.

ADOBONG TAKLONG (FOREST SNAILS)

President Roxas, Capiz

Taklong is a species of snail found in the woods or forested areas in Capiz and nearby provinces, mostly in moist places near rivers and waterfalls. Locals prepared this dish and served it during our kamayan-style picnic lunch at Liktinon Falls in Brgy. Bayuyan, President Roxas (along with more tame offerings like liempo & steamed crab). When cooked, the cut up pieces of snail meat resembled chicken gizzard or mussels.

Mango Pizza

Jordan Guimaras

The province of Guimaras is very famous for its mangoes. Every year they even have a festival dedicated to this fruit, which is why it isn’t a surprise that the locals have also incorporated this fruit into pizza, a dish that all Filipinos love. Mango Pizza originated in Guimaras province.The different flavors within the pizza plus the different textures that mix into one another make this a local favorite. The pizza is topped with thinly sliced Guimaras Mangoes, cheese, dizzled with crushed cashew bits and topped wth sliced green peppers.

La Paz Batchoy

Iloilo City, Iloilo

This steaming bowl of savory noodle soup was supposedly made of meat parts vendors weren’t able to sell that day. This explains why it contains a hodgepodge of ingredients, like bits of pork liver, pork intestines, thin beef slices, and bone marrow, all cooked in a robust pork and beef broth then garnished with egg, crushed chicharon, scallions, fried garlic, and even bagoong (guinamos in Hiligaynon).

Tinu-om

Cabatuan, Iloilo

Tinu-om is another iteration of native chicken that Iloilo is known for. This Cabatuan signature consists of boiled native chicken seasoned with onions, tomatoes, and lemongrass then wrapped in banana leaves, hence the name, which in the native dialect means “balot.” It’s served in tummy-warming broth with a mouthwatering aroma that balikbayan Ilonggos always crave upon landing home.

Piaya

Bacolod, Negros Occidental

The original crispy piaya is not quite the same as the piaya you'll find on supermarket shelves. Traditional piaya is a simple but tasty treat made of flour and muscovado (unrefined sugar). It is much thinner, almost wafer-like, and coated with just the right amount of muscovado.

Lumpia Ubod

Silay, Negros Occidental

It has been a hit with locals and tourists alike for years, is responsible for the best lumpia ubod in town. These fresh spring rolls are stuffed with ubod (heart of coconut), shrimp, pork, and green onions, and are both healthy and hearty. But what makes this delicacy truly stand out is the secret sauce that’s been passed down through generations.
